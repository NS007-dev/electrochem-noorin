What Is Electrochemistry?

Electrochemistry is the branch of chemistry that studies the relationship between chemical reactions and electrical energy. In electrochemical processes, chemical reactions cause electrons to move, and this movement of electrons can either produce electricity or use electricity to drive reactions.

The Basics (SCH4U Foundations)

Electrochemistry is based on redox reactions, which are reactions involving the transfer of electrons.

  • Oxidation is the loss of electrons
  • Reduction is the gain of electrons

Oxidation and reduction always happen together. This is why they are called redox reactions.

Electrochemical Cells

An electrochemical cell is a device that uses redox reactions to convert chemical energy into electrical energy or vice versa. There are two main types studied in SCH4U:

  • Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells: Use spontaneous redox reactions to generate electricity
  • Electrolytic Cells: Use electrical energy to force non-spontaneous reactions to occur

Electron Flow and Current

In electrochemical cells, electrons always flow from the anode to the cathode through an external circuit. This flow of electrons is what we observe as an electric current.

  • The anode is where oxidation occurs
  • The cathode is where reduction occurs

Electrochemistry in Real Life

Electrochemistry is used in many real-world applications, including batteries, fuel cells, corrosion, electroplating, and metal extraction. Understanding electrochemistry allows scientists and engineers to design cleaner energy sources, prevent material damage, and develop safer technologies.

This foundation of electrochemistry is essential for understanding how hydrogen fuel cells work and why they are considered a potential alternative source of power.

Electrochemistry Behind Hydrogen Fuel Cells (SCH4U Level)

Hydrogen fuel cells are an application of electrochemical cells, specifically galvanic (voltaic) cells. In a galvanic cell, a spontaneous redox reaction is used to convert chemical energy directly into electrical energy.

Oxidation and Reduction

Fuel cells rely on a redox reaction. Oxidation and reduction always occur together:

Cell Potential and Efficiency

Hydrogen fuel cells have a relatively high cell potential, which means they can convert a large amount of chemical energy into electrical energy. Unlike combustion engines, fuel cells are not limited by heat losses, making them much more efficient.

Environmental and Practical Challenges

Although fuel cells produce only water as a direct product, there are still major challenges:

  • Most hydrogen is produced using fossil fuels, which involves oxidation of hydrocarbons and produces CO‚ÇÇ
  • Hydrogen must be stored under high pressure, creating safety concerns
  • Catalysts such as platinum are required to increase reaction rate, making fuel cells expensive

These issues must be solved before hydrogen fuel cells can be widely used in transportation.
