Electrochemistry Foundations

The Basics (SCH4U)

Electrochemistry is based on redox reactions, meaning reactions where electrons are transferred. The key idea is that when electrons move, we can capture that movement as electric current.

  • Oxidation = loss of electrons
  • Reduction = gain of electrons

Oxidation and reduction always happen together. That‚Äôs why we call them redox reactions.

Electron Flow and Current

In electrochemical cells, electrons flow from the anode to the cathode through an external circuit.

  • Anode = oxidation
  • Cathode = reduction

Electrochemistry Behind Hydrogen Fuel Cells (SCH4U Level)

Hydrogen fuel cells are basically a galvanic (voltaic) cell. A spontaneous redox reaction releases energy, and instead of losing it as heat, the fuel cell forces electrons to travel through a circuit so we can use the energy as electricity.

Why fuel cells can be efficient

A fuel cell converts chemical energy to electrical energy more directly than a combustion engine, so it can avoid some heat losses. The problem is not the chemistry, it‚Äôs the real-world engineering, safety, and infrastructure needed to make it practical.

Hydrogen fuel cells in cars: what still needs to be solved? (click)

What people don‚Äôt realize about ‚Äúonly water‚Äù

Fuel cells are always advertised like ‚Äúthe only product is water,‚Äù which is true at the tailpipe, but there are still problems before they can fully replace gas cars. The biggest one is hydrogen storage. Hydrogen doesn‚Äôt take up a lot of mass, so to store enough for a car, you usually need really high pressure tanks. Another issue is just basic convenience. If there aren‚Äôt enough refuelling stations, people won‚Äôt trust it for daily driving. It‚Äôs the same reason electric cars needed charging stations before they became more common.

Chemistry connection (SCH4U)

Fuel cells work because of redox. Hydrogen is oxidized at the anode and oxygen is reduced at the cathode. The whole point is forcing electrons through the wire to make current.

But reaction rate matters. Catalysts help speed the reaction up, and if catalysts wear out or get ‚Äúpoisoned‚Äù by impurities, the fuel cell performance drops.

What actually helps

  • safer storage + better tank design
  • more refuelling stations
  • cleaner hydrogen production
  • longer lasting catalysts (so it‚Äôs cheaper)

Basically, the chemistry is solid. The hard part is making the whole system practical, safe, and actually low-emission overall.

Battery disposal: small habit, big safety effect (click)

Why you can‚Äôt just throw them out

Batteries shouldn‚Äôt go in regular trash or normal recycling. Some batteries can short-circuit if terminals touch metal, and that can literally start fires in recycling facilities. Also, damaged batteries can leak chemicals or metals.

The best method is using battery drop-offs (like municipal hazardous waste programs or battery recycling bins). A simple safety step is taping the terminals, especially for lithium-ion and 9V batteries.

Real-world impact

  • Safety: reduces facility fires
  • Environment: reduces toxic leaching and recovers metals
  • Society: normalizes responsible tech habits

Practical action

A battery drop box at school + a reminder poster is honestly an easy fix that actually helps.

Corrosion: slow chemistry that can turn into a safety problem (click)

Why corrosion is a big deal

Corrosion doesn‚Äôt look dramatic at first, but it‚Äôs serious because it weakens metal over time. Bridges and road structures depend on metal strength (steel supports or rebar). If corrosion keeps happening, it can lead to cracking, pieces breaking off, and in extreme cases, failure.

In winter, road salt makes it worse because it helps ions move. That basically increases conductivity, so the electrochemical corrosion process speeds up.

Chemistry connection

Rusting is a redox process. Iron gets oxidized, oxygen gets reduced, and water/electrolytes help the reaction continue. Prevention works by blocking oxygen/water (coatings), using different materials (alloys), or changing the electrochemistry (like cathodic protection).

Practical action

Regular inspections + better coatings + safe handling of chemicals (ventilation/PPE) so prevention doesn‚Äôt create new health issues.

Electroplating waste: why industrial chemistry needs control (click)

What the waste can involve

Electroplating uses metal ions in solution, so the waste can include wastewater and sludge with heavy metal ions (like nickel compounds and chromium compounds depending on the process). If it isn‚Äôt treated properly, those metals can end up in soil and water.

There‚Äôs also a worker safety side: fumes, splashes, and chemical handling. Even if something is ‚Äúfine‚Äù in a controlled lab, industrial scale needs strict rules.

What should happen

Wastewater should be treated properly (neutralization/precipitation/filtration depending on what‚Äôs in it), and companies should be routinely monitored. It costs money, but it prevents way bigger damage later.

This is a situation where technology benefits society (plating improves corrosion resistance), but it must be managed so the environment and workers aren‚Äôt the ‚Äúhidden cost.‚Äù

E-waste: when technology becomes toxic (click)

What escapes from e-waste

E-waste can release toxic stuff like lead, mercury, and cadmium. It gets worse when electronics are burned or taken apart improperly because toxins can get into air, soil, and water.

Health effects depend on exposure, but heavy metals can harm the nervous system (especially in kids) and cause long-term issues like kidney damage. So it‚Äôs not just ‚Äúbad for the planet‚Äù, it‚Äôs also a public health issue.

What needs to change

We need more collection programs and people actually using them. Also, companies should design electronics that are easier to recycle (or at least easier to take apart safely).

Electrochem connection

We use metals in devices for conduction and electrodes, but those same metals become harmful when they leak into ecosystems.

Why braces don‚Äôt rust (or rust very slowly) (click)

Corrosion resistance in the mouth

Braces are usually stainless steel or titanium alloys. They don‚Äôt corrode much because they form a protective layer on the surface called a passivation (oxide) layer. That layer blocks the metal underneath from reacting further.

The mouth is actually a pretty tough environment because saliva acts like an electrolyte, but these alloys are chosen because their oxide layers stay stable, so corrosion is very slow.

Chemistry connection

Corrosion is basically an unwanted redox cell happening on the metal surface. Passivation reduces electron transfer, so the ‚Äúcorrosion reaction‚Äù can‚Äôt keep going the same way.

Small practical note

Keeping good oral hygiene matters too because strong acidity can make corrosion more likely.
